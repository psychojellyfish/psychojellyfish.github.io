What's Dave Watching? - And Then There Were None

Submitted by Dave on Thu, 02/09/2017 - 21:50

And Then There Were None (1945)

Directed by Renè Clair

Witten by Dudley Nichols from a novel by Agatha Christie

Starring Barry Fitzgerald, Walter Huston, Louis Hayward, Roland Young, June Duprez, Mischa Auer, C. Aubrey Smith, Judith Anderson, Richard Haydn, and Queenie Leonard

Summary

Ten people are mysteriously gathered at an island mansion were their secrets are revealed and they begin to die off one by one in accordance to a morbid song.

My Perspective

I have known about this Agatha Christie title since I discovered the author’s work in high school, though I have not read the book. It was drawn further to my attention when it was brought up in relation to Tarantino’s The Hateful Eight. I assumed it had been adapted in some form, but had no knowledge of this films existence until I stumbled across it on TCM. My initial expectations were low, assuming that as it hadn’t shown up on my radar before it might have been a flop. It is, in fact, a classic masterpiece that has somehow fallen to obscurity despite all the reasons it should be remembered.

The ensemble cast is well chosen. The lack of major stars adds to the mystery where a too familiar face would give the viewer expectations as to its conclusion. Solid performers such as Huston and Fitzgerald do take center stage for much of the film, but the way their roles play off each other finds dramatic balance rather than overpowering the picture, and it also compliments the ever changing focus of suspicions. Young does a fairly memorable turn as the bumbling detective whose deductions are comically off the mark. Anderson and Haydn add tremendously to the dramatic tension, whereas C. Aubrey Smith brings a somber presence as the senile, possibly dementia suffering, elderly general.

The personalities make the picture. One of Christie’s great talents was to make characters so engaging the motives of the crime became obscured until the climactic reveal. In adapting the author’s style they hit the nail on the head, though maybe a bit too much on the mark. The performances, while highly entertaining, are big, boarding on caricature, and the premise, while oozing with dark mischief, is admittedly contrived. It’s not to be taken seriously, nor presented in any way as realistic. This does not diminish how fun and fantastic the film is, but is largely the reason why.

 The proceedings all together are rather stagey. This story, perhaps, is better suited for the stage than the screen. I can’t say that the film has any inherent advantage over a stage production of the same material. I can say that artistically it does have an interesting cinematic style. While in the era of noir and incorporating similarly dramatic black and white lighting, it is not noir. Stylistically it has more in common with gothic romance. Not as exact as Hitchcock’s Rebecca, which is a gothic romance and a noir, but in a way more ethereal. Images like the stark outline of figures on a windswept beach; a man outside a woman’s balcony in the moonlight; an unwound ball of yarn leading to a bedroom where a dead body lays. An overall atmosphere of dreamlike, whimsical mystery pervades the imagery of the film.

I believe anyone who watches this film and really engages with it will find it pleasing. It is a classic without having the official stamp of classic on it. It is a darkly humorous farce, a gothic romance, and an artistic oddity. Best of all, if you have no prior knowledge of the story, it’ll genuinely keep you guessing until the end.
