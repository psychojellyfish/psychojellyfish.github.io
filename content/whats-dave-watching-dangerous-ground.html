What's Dave Watching? - On Dangerous Ground

Submitted by Dave on Sat, 02/25/2017 - 15:54

On Dangerous Ground (1951)

Directed by Nicholas Ray and uncredited Ida Lupino

Written by A. I. Bezzerides from a novel by Gerald Butler

Starring Ida Lupino, Robert Ryan, Ward Bond, Charles Kemper, Ed Begley, and Sumner Williams

Summary

City cop Jim Wilson (Robert Ryan) is losing his grip and taking his frustrations out on the thugs he brings in. To help him cool off and get some perspective they give him a case out in the country. But when Jim faces a child killer and the victim’s vengeful father will he uphold the law, or continue to vent his outrage at the world?

My Perspective

When I saw in the opening credits it was written by A. I. Bezzerides, who also wrote the script for one of my all time favorite film noirs, Kiss Me Deadly, I knew I had reason to get excited. Bezzerides had a certain knack for using brutality in a negative light to point up an underlying liberal message. Here he hits with the unnerving brutality, but the liberal message is far too heavy handed.

There’s tough faced Ryan, a frequent star of noirs, westerns, and war films, in the lead role. A reliable talent for playing compelling tough guys, of his numerous roles I’ve seen him in I’d say his villainous turn as Reno in Bad Day at Black Rock is my favorite. As the lead here he’s best when there’s violence in the air you’re not sure what he’s going to do next. In fact the best scenes in this picture rise from the tension of uncertainty about what the characters might do, and Ryan is responsible for most of them.

Of course the lovely Ida Lupino takes over the screen whenever she’s on it, with a hefty helping of close ups from the camera. She’s almost haunting as the lonely blind farm girl desperately trying to protect her mentally ill brother who committed the murder. What isn’t available from the films credits is that she also directed some of this film when Ray fell ill. Which scenes she did I don’t know, but for those interested in viewing her full talent as a director around this time should see another fine noir, The Hitch-Hiker. Her career also includes a fantastic performance opposite Bogart in High Sierra, the film which introduced and hooked me into film noir!

The acting is excellent. Scenes are often filled with uncertain tensions, ambiguity, perhaps even suggestions of things they would never be allowed to show at the time. Sure, it’s aged, but only a little. Take the scene were Ryan beats a suspect for information screaming, “You know you’re going to talk. Why do you make me do it?” It might be a little cheesy, but Ryan’s mad eyed and increasingly unhinged performance is still unsettling, not to mention the suspects nearly excited anticipation of the beating which – maybe I’m crazy – makes it all seem sexually charged.

The best scene is when Ryan finally confronts the child murderer in a country shack. It’s clear right from our first glimpse of the killer, only a boy in his late teens played by Sumner Williams, is mentally handicapped. Ryan’s character realizes this as well, and tries to calm the raving boy. As Ryan closes in we still can’t be sure if his intension is violent or if he was honest with Lupino when he promised to bring him in unharmed. We might never know as the victim’s father enters and tries to shoot the boy. Director Ray here, and throughout, displays much of the same talent that would go into his later masterpiece, Rebel Without A Cause. Any fan of that film should absolutely find this one.

The solid plot is marred only by some uneven pacing and heavy handed moralizing. The latter feels overtly preachy, whether it’s Ryan’s fellow officers reprimanding him after pulling him off a thug or the vengeful father too late regretting his behavior as a viewer it took me out of the action. The pace suffers somewhat from its dramatic shift from the city to the country. The opening third of the film is a taught thriller in the city, but when he goes out to the country the film comes nearly to a halt to introduce a new setting and scenario. The middle of the film is a chase scene that drags out until we finally meet Lupino in the isolated farmhouse. Lupino’s character is interesting and adds to the tension and the drama, but we spend a little too much time with her wandering around her house and establishing that she’s blind, which any viewer can discern within half a minute. These problems are persistent and can be irritating, but the film is still well worth watching.

It’s a surprising, smart thriller with troubled protagonists and a strong stance against violence and retribution. While at times tonally off and preachy it is thematically well constructed.
