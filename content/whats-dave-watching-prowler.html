What's Dave Watching? - The Prowler

Submitted by Dave on Mon, 12/26/2016 - 17:13

The Prowler (1951)

Directed by Joseph Losey

Written by Dalton Trumbo

Starring Van Heflin, Evelyn Keyes, and John Maxwell

Summary

A housewife calls the police after spotting a prowler at her bathroom window. The officer who arrives flirts with her and then begins dropping by when her husband is at work. He twists her emotions into a knot before enacting a plot to kill her wealthy husband and make it look like an accident. He then marries the housewife, but the honeymoon quickly spirals out of control.

My Perspective

Call it a sweet-tooth, call it a guilty pleasure, but whatever you call it I sure do dig Film Noir. These certified classics of the 1940s and ‘50s are a must for Cinephiles and Average Joes alike. Among the standout titles like The Maltese Falcon and Sunset Blvd. there is a rich field of lesser known hidden gems to be discovered by anyone who cares to scratch the surface. The Prowler is one of those gems.

The Prowler aims not only to brood in the dark underbelly of society like most noir, but to make your skin crawl with this queasiest of affairs. Heflin’s forceful seduction of Keyes pulls no punch and had me flinching at several moments. He is a brute who turns lust into a disease with greed his prime motivator. Worse is when she gives into him completely, allowing him to tie her up inside and drag her down into his wicked scheme.

What’s remarkable is that you never lose sympathy for Keyes character, even when she’s brought to her lowest. Surely without compassion for her the plot would lose tightrope tension and the audience would care nothing for the outcome. It is a credit to both the writing and the performance of Keyes. While not a great actor it’s hard to go wrong when the writing and character development is this good. She is solidly believable and compelling in her role, and turns out to be much more than a two dimensional victim.

Screenwriter Trumbo, acknowledged as one of the greats in his field, was blacklisted at the time and wrote this under a pseudonym. His apparent intention here was to depict an underlying class struggle whereby the underpaid cop Heflin resorted to devious means to achieve his goals. But that gets buried under the disturbing sexual tension of the drama. Though the theme of strong contrasts is visible and is the backbone of the film. His poverty and her wealth, his lust and her love, his deviousness and her naiveté. There is even a rich contrast between Heflin and his older cop partner, the loveable “Bud” played by Maxwell. In fact there is a kindness exhibited in the side characters of this picture which makes Heflin’s cruelty all the more deplorable, as if his is the one black soul in an otherwise warm, though imperfect, world.

The exact plot I will not spoil here, for one of the great thrills in watching this is the consistently unpredictable turns it takes. At first it appears obvious where it’s heading, and it almost bores, but soon you find yourself glued to it utterly perplexed at where it will end up. Things which seem insignificant, like the older cops rock collecting hobby, tie into the narrative in surprising ways. And it keeps you guessing right up to the fever pitched end.

On what looks to be a near shoestring budget with B level characters actors these filmmakers executed an above average picture which shatters both emotions and expectations. No doubt in large part to the Trumbo script, cynical even for the genre of Film Noir, which is saying something!

Highly recommend this for anyone and everyone, but particularly for those seeking a sleazy pulp narrative with substance.
