Dave's Movie Hall of Fame: February '18

Submitted by Dave on Fri, 02/16/2018 - 15:24

Rashomon (1950)

I hadn’t watched a Japanese movie that didn’t involve giant monsters when I picked this up. I very quickly became aware that I was experiencing one of the greatest movies I’d ever see. I’d actually read the short story “In a Grove” a year before and sought out the film from that. I was aware of the unusual structure of the story, the telling and retelling of of an incident from different perspectives, but I was not anticipating how richly that story was interpreted by director Akira Kurosawa. This is an incredibly beautiful film. Kurosawa, I soon found out, made many amazing films, and I confidently place him among the best directors of all time, as well as my personal favorite. It also stars Toshiro Mifune, who would often work with Kurosawa, and is one of my favorite actors despite not being able to understand his dialog without subtitles. In its time this movie introduced American audiences and audiences around the world to Japanese cinema. It introduced this film-fan to one of the most rewarding artists he ever encountered.  

 

Dracula (1931)

When I first watched this as a kid I was less impressed with it than the more exciting Frankenstein and Wolf Man movies. It wasn’t until I saw it as an adult that I was able to fully appreciate its weirdness, its atmosphere, and the powerful performance of Bela Lugosi. I have since read the source novel twice and seen numerous other film adaptations, and while each has something to lay claim to I find this to be overall the strongest version of the material after the novel. Each year, around late October, I end up watching the film again, and each time finding something new that catches my attention. It continues to intrigue and mystify me. Almost more than Lugosi I credit director Tod Browning, in my estimation one of the strangest filmmakers of all, with imbuing in the film an elusive, mezmeric quality which works on the viewers subconscious imagination. Perhaps this is also due to so much of the action happening off screen; and rumor has it some of the more disturbing scenes were cut out at the studio’s insistence. There are things we’re aware of yet never see, and many more things we’re not specifically aware of, yet feel their presence implicitly. This is the granddaddy of all horror films, and while not the first, it is definitively the model by which all great horror has followed.

 

The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari (1920)

My love of monster movies eventually led to me exploring the classic silent films like Phantom of the Opera and Nosferatu, which then led me to this. I didn’t go in blind, I had read a little about the surreal feature in a film book, and had even drawn the picture provided in that book in my highschool sketchbook. The next source that gave me an idea of what I was in for was the Rob Zombie music video for the song “Living Dead Girl” done completely in the style of the film. As both a movie buff and an artist I was drawn in by this films creativity. The shadows painted on the walls; the crooked windows, streets, and everything that had an almost Dr. Suessian structure; the dreamlike logic of the whole presentation held such strong imaginative elements I could not compare it to anything else I’d seen outside of animation. Perhaps a classic example of style over substance, but of such pronounced and fantastic style to make it unique and unforgettable experience.

 

Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles (1990)

Whatever it was that launched and sustained the popularity of this absurd concept likewise captured my imagination and has entertained me for as long as I can remember. At its peak we got one of the darkest and most violent children’s films ever made, and I would argue one of the best. Based more on the outrageous and edgy underground comic book than the zany cartoon, it presented a mature and high stakes adventure that the mainstream media was perhaps unprepared for. To be sure some it’s violence had to be toned down prior to release, otherwise there would’ve been an even higher body count. Depicting minors smoking cigarettes still makes the final cut, though. Stylistically it takes largely from film noir and imported martial arts movies, with heavy doses of lower budget grime and grit typical to films of the late 80s and early 90s. It also required the filmmakers to invent new techniques that greatly advanced what was possible in puppetry and animatronics. In it’s time it became the highest grossing independent film ever made. I was too young to enjoy it in its original theatrical run, but was still of an age where it was on standard rotation on VHS. It became clear to me the impression this film had on my generation when in college in the auditorium before a lecture someone put it up on the screen and there was an audible gasp. I looked around at a room full of pretentious teenage art students with smiles on their faces as the were momentarily brought back to early childhood.

 

Young Frankenstein (1974)

One of the funniest films ever made. Why the combination of funny and scary work so well together, I can’t say, but it’s sweeter than a Reese’s peanut butter cup. The jokes are dumb, yet timeless; making them highly quotable. There are too many wonderful moments to mention here, but “Puttin’ on the Ritz” always steals the show. There’s something hypnotically magical that got captured in that performance. Every cast member is remarkable in their roles, and Marty Feldman’s Igor - or is it “Eye-gor” - is a bountiful treat every moment he’s on camera. He seems to feed off and amplify the films strange energy, and everything he does is funny. In that performance alone he created one of the greatest characters to ever hobble across the screen. I was a child sitting in front of the tv with my parents behind me when I first saw it, and as much as I laughed at the jokes I was also drawn in by the mysterious gothic atmosphere created by the cinematography and art direction. As I got older the film only became funnier and more impressive a work of cinematic brilliance, a credited to both the vision of Gene Wilder and its dedicated execution by Mel Brooks.

 

*Bonus Short: Herman’s Happy Valley - The Munsters (1965)

A loveable American family… of monsters. From the art direction to the comic chemistry of Fred Gwynne, Al Lewis, and Yvonne De Carlo the unique show stood out for taking typical sitcom tropes and satirically turning them on their heads. As a monster kid, I ate it up. This episode sees Herman purchase a little vacation property from some con men that turns out to be a ghost town, and, naturally, they love it. The best part is when the con men try to make it seem like the place is haunted to get rid of them. Here we get a great inversion of the haunted house scenario as Herman runs into a dressed up ghost and skeleton and scares them off, leaving him, as always, amusingly befuddled by their sudden and speedy departure.
