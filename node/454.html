What's Dave Watching? - Suicide Squad

Submitted by Dave on Mon, 09/05/2016 - 16:09

Suicide Squad (2016)

Directed by David Ayer

Written by David Ayer

Starring Will Smith, Jared Leto, Margot Robbie

Summary:

In response to growing metahuman activities government operative Amanda Waller selects a team of super villains and forces them to cooperate in dangerous missions to take down metahuman terrorists. When an ancient witch possessing one of her agents goes rogue and raises her ancient witch brother to help her make this mystical doomsday machine thing the Squad is called into action. They're amoral, self interested, and antisocial, but they come together in the end to save the day.

My Perspective:

As a life-long comic reader I walk into these movies with a slightly different attitude than most. I’m eager to see the lore I’m well versed in brought to life on the screen, but I’m also going in to a movie made for people who don’t know the lore. As such, exposition and background information feels like treading through familiar territory for me, which might be pleasing or tedious depending how it’s done.

Pleasing and tedious is a good summation of my feeling towards the movie. I enjoyed the characters on the screen, and yet was largely bored with what they were doing and why they were doing it.

I won’t say I dislike Suicide Squad, it is a thoroughly enjoyable popcorn picture, but the strong presentation of the characters is undermined by a lackluster and frankly stupid plot.

Movies like this face the problem of having stakes which are too abstract for the audience to appreciate in any tangible way. The witch makes a big glowing, spinning thing that’s supposed to destroy all humans after it gets charged up, which conveniently takes half the movie. There’s nothing tangible in this for the audience to care about at all.

In The Dark Knight the Joker is going to blow up two boats. We see the people on those boats, see their fear, and can mentally and emotionally measure the weight in loss if the Joker isn’t stopped. There’s real tension because the filmmakers have shown us what’s at stake.  

I can also not approve of the obnoxiously obvious greatest hits channel this movie was set to.

Despite these detriments the cast comes off surprisingly well, carting the dead weight of the picture far enough into positive territory to make it worth the trip. Will Smith particularly should get credit as the only a character the audience could sympathize with and care about how his story resolves. Also, as much as Margot Robbie tried, Smith got the biggest laughs out of the audience I was in. If there’s a reason to revisit this picture later on it will be because of him.

Robbie’s Quinn is definitely a stand out performance, but stands out a bit too much. Clearly her character needs her own movie as here she felt a bit like Jimi Hendrix in The Beatles. The subplot of her and the Joker serves mostly as a distraction from the rest of the picture. You also don’t want a subplot to be potentially more interesting than the main story.

Jared Leto does a good Joker, and while I could have done without the tattoos I would like to see more of him. Honestly, as a comics fan, it’s significantly closer to the comic’s character than Heath Ledger’s version. He is a human shark laughing like a hyena from hell.

Boomerang is quirky and fun in just the right way. I’m largely indifferent to Diablo. Slipknot was there to prove that the bombs in their necks worked, which felt unnecessary. Flagg needed more depth. Katana is an interesting idea that goes nowhere. Waller is intimidating, but deflates greatly as the film progresses. I simply couldn’t understand anything Killer Croc said.

The only problem with the character, again a plot contrivance, is that they bond so quickly it’s beyond unrealistic. They’re calling each other friends, even family, within twenty-four hours of first meeting? It’s ridiculous, unbelievable, and leads to awkward lines like, “I already lost one family! I’m not going to lose this one!” What?!?

With a premise that borrows elements from The Dirty Dozen and Escape from New York and adds a host loved comic book characters fresh to the screen I had hoped for more.
