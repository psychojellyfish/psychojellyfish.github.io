What's Dave Watching? - Shinjuku Incident

Submitted by Dave on Sat, 09/03/2016 - 18:15

Shinjuku Incident (2009)

Directed by Tung-Shing Lee

Written by Tung-Shing Lee and Tin Nam Chun

Starring Jackie Chan, Naoto Takenaka, and Daniel Wu

Summary:

Chan stars as a poor Chinese man who illegally immigrates to Japan in search of an old flame. Finding work with other Chinese immigrants through his young cousin, Joe in the English subtitles, he picks up garbage in landfills and sewers, running when police arrive, until one day discovering his hometown crush married a yakuza boss. Chan concludes a life of crime is the only to get what he wants, so organizes his little gang towards this end. Joe, who lacks the stomach for criminal enterprise, gets the brunt of the abuse when they cross paths with the yakuza, eventually getting his hand chopped off and his face disfigured. Chan goes to get revenge and ends up saving a yakuza boss – the same one who married his sweetheart – from assassination. The yakuza boss offers Chan territory and power in return for bumping off his two rivals. Chan does. Prosperity at last! Except that all Chan’s old buddies turn into hardened gangsters behind his back. The next time we see Joe he’s a strung out, ghost-faced punker. Chan confronts his gang, but at the same time rival yakuza siege the place armed with swords. In the brutal raid that follows the Chinese gang is slaughtered, the yakuza boss killed, Joe commits suicide by overdose, and Chan gets away just long enough to hand over incriminating yakuza information to the police before he bleeds out in the same sewers he once worked in as an illegal immigrant.

My Perspective

Jackie Chan’s usually a lovable character, at least that’s what I grew up on, so when I found a movie where he wasn’t I was deeply intrigued.

Truthfully, he’s not so much a “Bad Guy” here. He’s a sympathetic, flawed character. His flaws include jealousy, trusting the wrong people, and not being able to see how deep into hot water his ass is, but that doesn’t make him evil, it makes him a screw up.

Seeing Jackie Chan do horrible things is a bit awkward, to say the least. It’s not that Chan fails to make you believe his character is capable of violence, but cold blooded murder seems too drastic for this likeable guy to agree to without a blink. Then to see him do it almost made me laugh. Chan’s reputation for lighter-hearted fare is detrimental. I’m sure the intention was for the audience to feel sad for his dark turn into a dispassionate killer, but the switch is arbitrarily flipped.

Daniel Wu’s Joe succeeds far better at this. The actions of Chan’s character brings on the downfall and corruption of his friends, and none start out more innocent and end up more polluted the Joe. The character he becomes is unrecognizable from where he starts. It’s heartbreaking. By the time Chan acknowledges it and tries to do anything it’s too late. His death is the rawest moment in the picture. It looks like something out of Saw, except with a character we care about. Chan’s reaction, bursting into tears on the floor, is the height of his dramatic performance.

Visual comparison to the film Saw isn’t bad. Shot on 35 mm Kodak, it gives an impression much like grainer cult films of the past. It’s not slick, or crisp in any way, and there’s a great depth to its black. This is an extremely dark film, and the 35 mm enhances that. It also keeps it from feeling like a film of a certain era. Knowing nothing about this someone might easily mistake it for a film from the nineties. In this case that’s in its favor.

Stylistic flourishes crop up now and then for scenes involving the yakuza, notably using zooms to great effect. On reflection it’s largely influenced by Scarface, though not in a way that reeks of imitation. Of course it’s not as well made as Scarface either; few films are.

Interesting to note there is a bit of English dialog to be heard in its un-dubbed version. A news broadcast shows a discussion about how the Yakuza operates, summed up by the interviewee proclaiming with a shrug, “It’s complicated.” This is an explicit message to English viewers that they’re not going to understand the yakuza situation if they don’t already, and the film isn’t going to take the time to explain. It got a laugh out of me.

It seems to me that Chan is playing a younger character than he is here. This is of minor note, but does make certain things feel a bit odd. Are Jackie and his cousin played by Wu supposed to be the same age? He looks old enough to play Wu’s father. Not to mention the actresses who play his love interests make it seem like he’s into younger women, but in a childhood flashback it’s clear they’re about the same age. The film makes no attempt to deal with this issue, it’s just there.

One moment I could have done without is where Jackie, having just learned his old girlfriend married a Japanese guy, cries while he has sex with a prostitute. Seriously, I could’ve lived a long, happy life without that.

The supporting cast does outstanding work. In particular Wu plays his changes excellently, if extreme, making his arch easily strongest part of the film. Bingbing Fan is a secondary love interest who offers Chan a way out of the madness, but doesn’t get nearly enough to do because, sadly, the film just drops her at a certain point. Kaoto Takenaka plays a dead perfect Inspector character that’s sympathetic to Chan, and does enough to suggest greater range than the film gives him screen time to explore.

Seeing Chan step away from his usual type to play a tragic character in a serious crime thriller is well worth the excursion. Even without his kung-fu physicality he is still an engaging performer, and is well supported here by other players. I feel it could be much better dramatically, had they chose to take it further in that direction, but it is primarily an action picture.
