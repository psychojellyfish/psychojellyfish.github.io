What's Dave Watching? - Across 110th Street

Submitted by Dave on Thu, 12/08/2016 - 17:19

Across 110th Street (1972)

Directed by Barry Shear

Written by Luther Davis from a Wally Ferris novel

Starring Anthony Quinn, Yaphet Kotto, Paul Benjamin, Ed Bernard, Antonio Fargas, Anthony Franciosa, and Norma Donaldson

Summary

Three nobodies hit a Mafia money exchange in Harlem and make off with three-hundred thousand dollars. With vengeful mobsters and cops each racing to find them first their chances of getting away are slim to none, and it doesn’t take long for them to get rooted out one by one.

My Perspective

A significant blaxploitation crime thriller with strong plotting and a longer list of developed characters than most any film you’re likely to see. I suspect this is in part due to the novel, off which it’s adapted, but good source material rarely translates to great film and immense credit is due to the filmmakers for reaching the level of depth that’s visible here. It’s fast paced and brutally violent, though one never feels it exceeds the bounds of reality, perhaps making it all the more disturbing.

Racial tensions play a large part thematically. It clearly has a message on its mind that threatens outright preaching at times, especially in scenes with Quinn and Kotto. The inner city conflict between Italian gangsters, black gangsters, the police, and the poor caught in the middle is a well drawn and rich setting, it makes Quinn and Kotto’s pointed confrontational banter come off hammed and unnecessary.

Stars Quinn and Kotto are in fact overshadowed by other castmates. Bernard, Fargas, and Paul Benjamin in particular are outstanding as the desperate trio who rob the mob in hopes of getting out of the ghetto for some better life. Benjamin’s character, Jim Harris, is a simpleton who’s done time and suffers seizures without medication. Life has scarred this man, and his feeble attempt to strike back is as heartbreaking as it is frightening. Donaldson, playing his love interest, experiences much of that heartbreak and fear as both a lover and something of a caring mother to Jim, and excels in the role.

Quinn, though overplaying his part, is not without merits. He convincingly portrays a cop who’s past his prime and knows it, filled with insecurities as he blusters and bullies. He’s an inflated brute whose self-recrimination lies always just under the surface, pleading for pity from others, such as the scene where he interrogates the dying Jackson (Fargas, best known as Huggy Bear from TV’s Starsky & Hutch) in the back of an ambulance. Quinn knows he’s wrong and when Jackson dies he deflates under the weight of Kotto’s accusing gaze. Kotto, a performer who needs little to be compelling, here feels little more than an overtly symbolic counterpoint to Quinn.

Director Shear worked largely in television and the film looks like an exceptionally stylish TV crime drama with the broadcast restraints taken off. The raw texture of 70’s era film is enhanced by real New York City locations, including Harlem. Mostly set a night, except, notably, for the ending which takes place in the day as they chase Benjamin across rooftops. This is a film where you feel like you’re there watching the actors sweat.

What marks this film as truly unforgettable is the amazing soundtrack by Bobby Womack. You’ll never be able to quit humming these funky tunes. Tarantino wisely used the title track of this film for the opening credits of his picture, Jackie Brown.

A well executed, dramatic thriller that rises above potboiler status with a surprising depth of character. It is engaging to view today without any disclaimer for its age. With or without the blaxploitation genre label it holds its own as a quality film worth remembering.

Recommended for fans of gritty crime flicks, weaker stomachs beware.
