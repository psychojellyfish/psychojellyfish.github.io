What's Dave Watching? - Shock Corridor

Submitted by Dave on Wed, 10/12/2016 - 16:15

Shock Corridor (1963)

Directed by Samuel Fuller

Written by Samuel Fuller

Starring Peter Breck, Constance Towers, Gene Evans

Summary

An ambitious, self interested reporter goes undercover as a patient in a mental institution to investigate a murder. As he interacts with the inmates, and is treated as one, he begins to exhibit their insane behavior. He succeeds in uncovering the story, but loses his mind in the process.

My Perspective

Having been a cinephile from an early age I find it troublesome that I am only now discovering the work of Samuel Fuller. That such a clear master of the craft has gone under my radar for so long is a shame, but as a cinephile it encourages me that there is more I have yet to explore and discover. For my tastes, this may be among the richest discoveries I’ve made.

To be fair, I’d come across mention of Shock Corridor as much as a decade ago, but only now picked up a copy; thanks again to my local library. My impression, from what little I heard about it back then, was that it was Camp. From the title, the image of the stripper girlfriend, the subject of a man going crazy in an asylum, it looked like Camp to my uninformed eye. I was mistaken.

While there is a great degree of fun to be had here, the tone and delivery is a blending of Lynchian surrealism and the toughest, most uncompromising Post-Noir Pulp you’re likely to find.

The performances are uncanny. I can’t quite put my finger on it. It isn’t method or classical. It’s like Fuller took actors trained to the stagey style of the time and pushed them into skin raw, powerful territory. This is not only true for the unhinged performances of the crazed inmates, but is also true for the protagonist’s concerned girlfriend who is convincingly and realistically horrified at seeing the madman her lover is becoming.

I’m left in awe at the force of will director Fuller seems to wield in the performances of his actors. Matched with his cinematography there is a sense of raw truth in every frame. You’re dealing with a creator who’s not going to bullshit you. As with every aspect of this film I consider I keep returning to the word uncompromising.

The next shock comes from the politics this film unexpectedly gets into. I wouldn’t call it a political film explicitly, but rather a film in which there are politics present. These are expressed with three inmates the protagonist gets close to as they are eyewitnesses to the murder. It’s intriguing the way the film shows sympathy towards the inmates, as well as the protagonist’s girlfriend, while it shows no sympathy for the driven, self interested protagonist. Still, the plot to uncover the murder mystery nearly takes a full backseat to the narrative of these characters’ experiences.

The first is a Korean War veteran who defected to the communists, was trained to brainwash American prisoners, but was converted back by a soldiers stories of American history. When he came back home he was persecuted for what he had done. Now he’s an inmate who believes he’s a Lieutenant in the Civil War. When we first meet him acting like TV cowboy soldier we laugh, when we hear his story we cease to laugh.

The second, and most compelling, is a black man who believes he is the founder of the Ku Klux Klan. If you didn’t just react in some way, reread that sentence. We learn he attended a white school and was put through so much pressure to succeed and abuse from racists that he cracked. This character’s presence is astonishing and cathartic as hell. It points up both the absurdity and horror of racism without ever coming off as pedantic. The actor seems to be fully enjoying his part as well as he puts on a hood and shouts lines like, “Get that N***** before he marries my daughter!”

The third is an atomic scientist so scarred by the implications of his work that he’s reverted to the mentality of a child. This reminds me of an episode of The Twilight Zone in which elders find a place where they become children again. He busies himself with coloring and gives a perfect impression of childish innocence, then, from that figure of innocence, speaks the horror of atomic war. Innocence lost.

Each of the inmates reveals their stories in moments of clarity where they are their old selves again. Their stories are accompanied by color stock footage which contrasts the black and white narrative and subtly takes us into the territory of an art film. As the covering of their insane character is peeled away and their humanity shown the tragedy is realized. The protagonist, however, only cares about finding the answers to his murder mystery, and pressing them drives them back into the dark ignorance of their madness. It is evocative and heartbreaking.

The tension builds to a fever pitch when the protagonist finally breaks. The corridor where all the inmates congregate is suddenly empty, and then a thunderstorm bursts in with rain pouring down on him. It’s a stunning visual piece sure to be seared into the minds of anyone who watches this.

 

For an obscure hidden gem this turned out to be one of the most powerful films I’ve seen. The voice of its creator, Samuel Fuller, is strong in every moment. It’s an uncompromising masterwork of cinema wrapped in a low budget veneer. I’m still in shock.
