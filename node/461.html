What's Dave Watching? - Blast of Silence

Submitted by Dave on Sat, 10/01/2016 - 17:31

Blast of Silence (1961)

Directed by Allen Baron

Written by Allen Baron and Waldo Salt

Starring Allen Baron, Molly McCarthy, and Larry Tucker

Summary

A hired gun comes to New York for a contract on a mob boss. As he coolly stalks his prey he happens across some old friends from the orphanage where he grew up. The experience shakes his sense of detachment and makes him question what his life has become and what it could be. He goes through with the mob hit intending it as his last job, but instead of payment he gets shot in the back.

My Perspective

Don’t be surprised if you’ve never heard of it, I certainly hadn’t. It’s an obscure picture from a director whose work almost entirely covers episodes of TV shows from the ‘60s and ‘70s; this being the notable exception. I discovered it on the shelf at my local library, my eye drawn to the Criterion logo on the box.  

I make special note to check out any odd, late period Noir pictures when I come across them. Odd usually means low budget, or foreign, and late period means it came out somewhere between 1950 and the early ‘60s, post the heyday of Noir and into the atomic-age. Films like Gun Crazy, D.O.A. with Edmond O’Brien, Stanley Kubrick’s The Killing, and Kiss Me Deadly. It’s iffy whether anything after the ‘60s can legitimately be called Noir, but that’s not an issue here.

Low budget, often seen as a detriment, can often become an enriching X-factor for the creatively ambitious. What stand out the most here are the real locations around New York and Long Island. Not only is it an interesting time capsule, it adds depth, texture, and realism to the setting that can’t be achieved on a set. A grounding influence in a genre which leans towards surrealism. At times convincing the audience of the narrative where the performers fall short.

We get long tracking shots following Allen Baron down city streets, shots looking down from rooftops, lived in interiors, and an astonishingly desolate looking finale shot on Long Island during an actual hurricane. This film is great to watch just for what they got on camera.

The acting isn’t pro, but it isn’t phoned in either. Good cinematography and real locations help sell it, but there are static shots of characters talking that go on long enough you start to look at your watch or wonder if you need to get laundry done. Contrasting this are emotional peaks in the story whenever Baron is around the woman he grew up with who he still holds a torch for. First when she gets him to open up at a party, then, when they’re alone, where he tries to rape her, than when he apologizes for the attempted rape, and finally when he goes to confess his passion for her and finds she’s committed to another man. All these scenes, though stagey, carry a weight of tension that no other interactions in the film have; not even the killings.  

The killings are particularly impressive in their own right, especially for how drawn out and brutal they get. Baron buys a black market gun from a large man who later puts together how big the contract hit is and blackmails him for more money. Baron grabs a fire ax from the hall and swings at the guy, misses, and gets into brawl with the hefty fellow where they tear up the whole apartment until Baron is able to strangle him with a lamp cord. It’s so much rougher than anything in film leading up to that point would suggest and it threw me off guard.

The contract hit itself isn’t nearly as violent, but the suspense leading up to it is heavy. Baron lays in wait in the mobster’s mistress’ apartment and shoots him with a silenced pistol. It’s almost, but isn’t the old fashion Hollywood killing where it’s bang and the guy falls down clutching his gut. Baron fires multiple shots and the mobster collapses backwards against the wall, the camera close up on his face as he checks out. Something about these kills reminds me of the violence in Orson Welles’ Touch of Evil, and it could easily have been an influence for this picture.

At the end Baron heads out to a windy field to collect his payment, realizes it’s a set up, tries to run, and gets shot in the back crawling through the mud. It’s a fitting, tragic end, and fantastic imagery.

The other interesting thing about the movie is its voice over narration. Now, usually I’m not a great fan of this as it can easily feel unnecessary or just be plain awkward. Here, however, it really does add a lot to the picture.  It is not a first person narrative, but a second person narrative. Instead of saying, I do this, or, I feel this way, it says, YOU do this, and, YOU feel this way. Hearing that repeatedly creates a direct connection between the viewer to the characters thoughts and experiences. You really have no reason to like or care about Baron’s character without this narration, but you do because it’s there. The tone of the film facilitates the effectiveness of this choice, which I doubt would work in almost any other circumstance, and it makes for a truly unique viewing.

Here is a movie with one foot set firmly in a trope laden genre which was about to run its course, and the other foot, perhaps accidentally by the nature of its production, set in a gritty realism that would come in the next generation of The French Connection and Chinatown on the path towards Martin Scorsese. An earnest and daring film, if rough around the edges, that’s still a solid watch today.
