Dave's Movie Hall of Fame: January '18

Submitted by Dave on Tue, 01/16/2018 - 15:59

Escape From New York (1981)

When I think of this film, which is fairly often, it’s totally about the attitude. It has a kind of punk nihilism, and no one did that better than Carpenter, not even Alex Cox, but it’s so over the top it’s funny. This film is almost a caricature of cool – Kurt Russell is doing a Clint Eastwood impression the entire movie, not to mention acting in scenes with Lee Van Cleef – and yet it never comes off like a joke. It feels like cinematic anarchy on the same level as The Marx Brothers; undercutting authority, breaking the rules and going as far with it as they possibly could. Seriously, watch Groucho’s “I’m Against It” bit from Horse Feathers and then watch this, I think you’ll see what I mean. I’m a big fan of Carpenter, something in his anarchic voice speaks to me, and this is him in top form. It also has one of my favorite scores ever; a synth masterpiece composed by the director himself.

Invasion of the Astro-Monster [Godzilla vs. Monster Zero] (1965)

A childhood favorite and possibly the most fun of the entire Kaiju franchise. While it’s larger than life star may only be in the film for about 20 minutes, they fill the rest of the time with a solidly entertaining Alien invasion plot. Men from Planet X make a deal to take away Earth’s monsters, only to bring them back as mind controlled forces of destruction. It’s got flying saucers, lasers, 3 giant monsters, and dub track. What more could you want?

Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade (1989)

It’s a great father and son adventure film, and, perhaps, for that reason my Dad and I watched this more than any other movie when I was growing up. We would quote the lines and reference it on a regular basis. It was like a part of the family. Also, my Dad actually fought the Nazis, so that added another level to it. I can’t look at this film without thinking of my Dad, and it brings back happy memories.

The Treasure of Sierra Madre (1948)

Some films are classics, some are a matter of taste, this is one of the essentials. If you haven’t seen it, you must. Director John Huston has an impressive filmography, and yet this tops the list. 2 guys go searching for gold with the help of an old prospector, brilliantly performed by the directors father, Walter Huston, and the more they get their hands on the more they go mad with greed. The paranoia is palpable as you watch these characters sweat and sleep with one eye open. Bogart is terrifying and fascinating to watch as he goes over the edge, it’s no wonder that many of his most memorable performances, other than Casablanca, were working with Huston.

2001: A Space Odyssey (1968)

Few films are as impressive as this. I know it gets a bad rap for being boring or you have to be stoned to enjoy it. Forget that. This is art. It’s pure cinema that pushed the boundaries of what was technically possible far beyond its time. It’s a challenging and thoughtful masterpiece that asks us to look beyond ourselves, beyond our time, to a vast universe of possibilities. When I watch it I’m taken out of myself into a transcendent state, and I don’t do drugs. It is simply the power of thought expressed through art.

*Bonus Short: Code of Hero – Beast Wars: Transformers (1998)

I remember watching this show every morning before school. My favorite character was Dinobot, who started as a villain, but defected to the heroes because he lived by a samurai-like code of honor. The series had a time travel premise and season 2 saw Dinobot struggling with the concept of fate vs. self determination – you know, kid’s stuff. In this episode, to save history, Dinobot must stand alone against all the villains in a badass showdown. He wins the fight, but dies from his injuries. The episode ends with the heroes performing his funeral.

When you’re 10 years old this stuff comes off like Hamlet. Something about this character, his arc and his end, while not sophisticated, it stuck with me. This wasn’t Bambi’s Mom who died in the 1st act, this character I followed every morning, had a kind of youthful affection for, and they killed him off the show. It contributed to my understanding and appreciation for stakes in stories. That the safety net protecting characters can be removed, and that that is interesting.
